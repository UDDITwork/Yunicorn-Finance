Stock Market Mechanism

A stock market is a place where companies raise capital by selling shares of stock (also known as 'equity') to investors. Most stocks give shareholders voting rights and a residual claim on corporate earnings in the form of capital gains and dividends. Stock markets also allow existing shares of publicly traded companies to change hands, where individuals and institutions can invest in companies to potentially profit from their success or speculate on shorter-term price movements. They play a crucial role in price discovery, as the collective actions of buyers and sellers determine the market value of companies. Moreover, stock markets promote corporate transparency and accountability, as public companies must adhere to regulatory standards and disclose financial information to shareholders. Through their functioning, stock markets serve as important economic indicators, reflecting investor sentiment and a barometer of overall economic health. When stock prices rise, it often signals investor confidence in the economy, while falling prices may indicate concerns about economic stability.The stock market allows investors to buy and sell shares of publicly traded companies. It operates through exchanges like the BSE (Bombay Stock Exchange) and NSE (National Stock Exchange) in India. Investors earn profits through dividends or by selling shares at a higher price than they were purchased. The stock market is a barometer of the economy's health and is influenced by various factors, including economic indicators, market sentiment, and global events.

BSE and NSE

The Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE), established in 1875, is the oldest stock exchange in Asia, and the National Stock Exchange (NSE), founded in 1992, is the largest in terms of daily trading volume. Both play a vital role in the Indian economy, providing a platform for companies to raise capital and for investors to trade securities. While BSE has a longer history, NSE introduced electronic trading and brought about transparency and efficiency in the market. Both exchanges are regulated by the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI).

KEY TAKEAWAYS

  • Stocks represent ownership equity in a firm, and many give shareholders voting rights, as well as a residual claim on corporate earnings in the form of capital gains and dividends.
  • Individual and institutional investors come together on stock exchanges to buy and sell shares in a public venue.
  • Share prices are set by supply and demand as buyers and sellers place orders.

How Do Stock Markets Work?

  • Stock markets are organized platforms where buyers and sellers come together to trade shares of publicly listed companies. At their core, these markets operate on the principle of supply and demand, with share prices fluctuating based on companies' perceived value and overall market conditions. When more people want to buy a stock than sell it, the price typically rises, while the opposite is true.
  • Stock markets facilitate both a primary and a secondary market. The primary market is where new stocks are first issued through initial public offerings (IPOs). Here, companies sell shares directly to investors, raising capital for business operations or expansion. Once these shares are in circulation, they enter the secondary market, where most daily trading occurs. In the secondary market, investors trade existing shares among themselves, with the company no longer directly involved in these transactions.

Stock exchanges, such as the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) or NASDAQ, provide the infrastructure for these trades to take place. They maintain orderly markets, ensure compliance with regulations, and disseminate real-time price information. Modern stock markets largely operate electronically, with sophisticated computer systems matching buy and sell orders almost instantaneously.

Various market participants play important roles in the functioning of stock markets. These include:

  • Individual and institutional investors (like mutual funds and pension funds) who own shares.
  • Traders who buy and sell over short time horizons.
  • Market makers (MMs) who provide liquidity.
  • Brokers who facilitate trades.
  • Additionally, regulatory bodies oversee market operations to protect investors and maintain market integrity. Together, these elements create a complex ecosystem that enables efficient capital allocation and provides opportunities for investment and wealth creation.

How Do Stocks Work?

When a company wants to raise capital, it divides what it believes to be the total amount of capital needed into slices of ownership with a specific value. These slices are called shares and usually represent a percentage of ownership in a company. Commonly, but not always, these shares come with voting rights and the potential to receive a portion of the company's profits through dividends or capital appreciation. The shares issued by the company to shareholders are called outstanding shares because investors, not the corporation, hold them.

The total value of a company's outstanding shares is known as its market capitalization, which fluctuates based on the current stock price and the number of shares issued.

Market capitalization rises and falls with the current market price of the shares and reflects the value placed on a company by the market.

Why Stock Markets Are Important

Stock markets are integral to modern economies. They provide companies with access to capital by allowing them to sell shares to the public, enabling businesses to fund growth, innovation, and expansion. The influx of capital received from IPOs drives economic development, job creation, and technological advancement, benefiting society as a whole.

Beyond capital raising, stock markets facilitate the efficient allocation of resources. They do this through the price discovery process, where the prices of stocks are determined in real time by the collective actions of buyers and sellers. This mechanism helps ensure that capital is directed towards the most promising and productive enterprises. Investors, through their buying and selling decisions, signal which companies they believe will succeed and grow—and those which may not. This dynamic process helps allocate resources to their most efficient uses, promoting overall economic efficiency.

For investors, stock markets provide opportunities to grow wealth over time. By purchasing shares, individuals and institutions can participate in the financial successes (or failures) of companies. Access to stock markets allows a broad range of people to build financial security and work towards long-term goals such as retirement or education funding. Today, many individuals rely on stock investments to fund future goals such as retirement savings, college tuition, and even healthcare spending.

Stock markets also serve as barometers of broad economic health and investor sentiment. Market indices, such as the S&P 500 or Dow Jones Industrial Average, provide snapshots of overall market performance, often reflecting broader economic trends and expectations. Policymakers, businesses, and individuals frequently use these indicators to inform decision-making, from monetary policy and business strategies to personal financial planning.

Major Stock Exchanges

  • Stock exchanges date back to the 17th century, with the Amsterdam Stock Exchange, established in 1602, often cited as the world's first formal stock market.
  • However, the concept of trading company shares existed even earlier, with the Roman Republic having a system for trading shares in publicani companies.
  • As commerce and industry grew, so did the need for organized markets to facilitate the trading of securities.
In the United States, the first U.S. stock exchange was the Philadelphia Stock Exchange (PHLX), founded in 1790, which still exists today.

The more famous New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) dates back to 1792, when 24 brokers signed the Buttonwood Agreement, establishing rules for trading securities. The NYSE has since grown to become one of the world's largest and most influential exchanges. The second major U.S. exchange, NASDAQ, was founded in 1971 as the world's first electronic stock market. Today, these two exchanges dominate the U.S. landscape, with the NYSE known for its auction-based trading system and blue-chip stocks, while NASDAQ is home to many technology and growth companies.

Internationally, several major exchanges also exist. The London Stock Exchange, founded in 1801, is one of Europe's oldest and largest. In Asia, the Tokyo Stock Exchange, established in 1878, is Japan's primary bourse, while the Shanghai Stock Exchange, reopened in 1990 after a long hiatus, has become increasingly important as China's economy has grown.

Other significant exchanges include the Hong Kong Stock Exchange, Euronext (which operates markets in Amsterdam, Brussels, Dublin, Lisbon, London, Oslo, and Paris), and the Frankfurt Stock Exchange.

In most developed countries, stock exchanges are formed as self-regulatory organizations (SROs), nongovernmental organizations that have the power to create and enforce industry regulations and standards. The advent of electronic trading has revolutionized stock exchanges worldwide. Starting with NASDAQ's electronic system in the 1970s, most major exchanges have now either fully transitioned to electronic trading or operate hybrid systems. This shift has dramatically increased trading speed and efficiency, reduced costs, and improved market access for a wider range of participants. It has also facilitated the rise of alternative trading systems (ATS) and dark pools, challenging traditional exchanges and reshaping the global financial landscape.

FACT CHECK

  • The original method of stock market trading was based on a manual system known as the open outcry system, where traders used verbal and hand signal communications to buy and sell large blocks of stocks in the trading pit or the exchange floor.

    • Over-the-Counter Exchanges

  • There also exist several loosely regulated over-the-counter (OTC) exchanges, which may also be referred to as bulletin boards (OTCBB). Shares listed OTC tend to be riskier since they list companies that fail to meet the more strict listing criteria of bigger exchanges. Larger exchanges may require that a company has been in operation for a certain amount of time before being listed and that it meets certain conditions regarding company value and profitability.
